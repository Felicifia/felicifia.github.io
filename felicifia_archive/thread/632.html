I've found it pretty easy to give up things that I've spent a lot of time on. In grade school and junior high I was into chess, but I haven't played a game in years. I was into juggling for a longer time, maybe ten years, though I wasn't serious about it. I haven't juggled since I graduated from college, where there was a juggling club.

I might be able to do things that are more productive (in terms of increasing the welfare of others) than music, but that are similarly enjoyable. I remember hearing somewhere (I think it was in Stumbling on Happiness) a relevant (fictional) story.
There was this professional cellist. And he got his thumb stuck in a blender, or whatever, and he couldn't play the cello anymore. For six months he was depressed. And then he figured out that there was more to life than playing the cello. There were other things he was good at, and other things he enjoyed doing. So his mood returned to its previous level, though his missing thumb attracted stares.

I think not playing the piano would be pretty easy, since it's not central to my identity, and there are other things I enjoy doing. Sorry for going off-topic!

That reminds me of another habit I have: reading self-help books and then not changing anything about the way I behave.

by Pat on 2012-07-05T23:39:00

I agree with Arepo. I didn't mean to suggest that we should give up all leisure activities. My point was narrower. Let's say you're deciding whether to engage in activity X or activity Y. X and Y are equally enjoyable, but Y is more productive. Y is probably the better choice, even if you've invested a lot of time in X. In my case, it might make more sense to exercise more and play the piano less, since both activities are enjoyable, but exercise would probably make me more energetic, while playing the piano wouldn't have this extra benefit.

If you've invested a lot of effort in something, deciding to give it up is hard. But if you do, you might be surprised at how little you miss it. I played the clarinet in a school band for 7 years. After I graduated, I stopped playing it, and I didn't miss it. I probably won't give up playing the piano, but I might practice half an hour a day instead of a full hour.

One thing that I don't understand is why many lawyers and investment bankers work 80 hours or more a week. Are they superhuman, unaffected by lack of sleep, exercise, and leisure time? Do their workplace cultures motivate them, so that even somebody of ordinary willpower and stamina could survive for a time in such a job? Are long hours just part of the culture, so it doesn't matter that the workers are not especially productive?
