Traditional eugenics

by Elijah on 2013-01-07T21:54:00

I have been thinking about the concept of 'traditional' eugenics. Note that I define 'traditional eugenics' as any policy that regulates reproductive freedom (though not necessarily coercive). Thus, China's one-child policy is 'traditionally eugenic', but genetic screening is not.

Presently, genotypic levels of 'g' are in severe decline (cf. Woodley, 2012, The social and scientific correlates of genotypic intelligence and the Flynn effect). This is problematic not just because it compromises rates of innovation and economic productivity, but because it enforces tribalism. The less intelligent are more tribalistic––more nationalistic and racist (and likely more speciesist, although I know of no studies on this in particular)––even when controlling for all the obvious confounds. A genetic screening method of eugenics requires a high mean level of population 'g' to get off the ground, so it is untenable until 'g' has been raised significantly.

What should we do? The segment of the population that experiences superreplacement fertility is in the IQ band of 75-90. Traditional eugenic policies have not targeted this segment (IQ 75-90 )of the population, but the mentally retarded. William Shockley proposed two non-coercive 'traditional' policies; in one of them, he proposed a payment of $1,000 per IQ point below 100 to anyone who agreed to be sterilized (adjusted for inflation, more like $5,000). The risk here is that such a policy would lead to the dehumanization of this segment of the population and a view of them as 'expendable'. It is also arguable that people at the low end of this range (IQ 75-80) simply don't have the capacity to make such a decision. But I think the long-term gain in utility outweighs the short-term loss.

What do you guys think?
