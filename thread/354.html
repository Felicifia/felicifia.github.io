I have a few comments on the proposed study and some examples of existing research that shows something similar, but first....

This forum would be more focussed on a discussion of why it is that you (and others) believe that consumerism is a crime against nature. I am familiar with models of ecologically responsible behavior, so I tend to describe such issues from a psychological viewpoint, but the challenge of changing such behavior through the philosophy of this forum would probably include a demonstration of the many ways that the natural environment benefits humans or perhaps how nature has intrinsic value that is important to humans.

As for why people fail to see the value in nature, I could suggest many things, one of which is the preoccupation with consumerism and other worldly pursuits that stem, in part, from a failure to understand the invalidity of social value placed on socio-economic status. In other words, from the philosophy of utilitarianism, I would say that people cannot simply start weighing utility of nature because they begin the analysis with faulty ideas that they adopted from their cultural surrounding without the scrutiny of reason.

As for the study you described, my first comment is that in many places you mention "emotion" when the study itself addresses only "empathy." People can have many emotions while absorbed in the consumerist delusion: envy, fear, jealousy, anger.... Unfortunately, envy, fear (of somebody discovering that you really aren’t cool), jealousy, and the like do not produce behaviors that are good for humans in the long run. Empathy is useful because it can lead to prosocial behavior and ecologically responsible behavior.

Another way to explore the proposed study is to first review the mountains of research that shows that perspective-taking increases empathy, which increases altruisitic behavior, then show that people in certain modes of existence fail to take the time to consider other people (or animals or nature). Here is one study that might be related to your topic. The general idea is that when people misunderstand the origins of their emotions, they respond differently to those emotions. Coke, Batson, and McDavis (1978) played a recording of a radio program about a girl whose parents died in a car crash. Some were then asked to imagine how the girl felt and some reviewed broadcasting techniques (as a control treatment). Before the study, participants were given a pill, half were told that it would relax them and half were told that it would arouse them. The results showed that those in the relaxation condition gave much more money than those in the arousal condition, and those who imagined the girls situation have the most (Coke et al., 1978, p. 756). In other words, when people mistakenly thought that their emotional experience was caused by the pill and not their feelings of empathy, they did not respond in the appropriate manner. The link to your study might be that people who live hectic lives are under stress (or other emotional influences) fail to properly identify emotions that are really caused from empathy or something like it. Their second study was similar but used electrodes and false feedback to tell (randomly) some people that they had a strong emotional response to the story about people dying in the car crash. Those who were (randomly) told that they had a strong emotional impact gave more money to help (because of they way they interpreted their alleged emotional reaction).

There was another study that I can’t find right now, but the general idea was this. Students were given a task to take something across campus. Some were told that they had to get to the other building as fast as possible and others were given plenty of time. Those who were under time pressure were much less likely to help a stranger. I think this study was from the 1970s or 1980s, perhaps by Cialdini, Batson, Shalom Schwartz, or Dovidio, and it might have focussed on the effects of egoistic concern or stress on altruistic behavior or helping behavior (if you find it, let me know). The point is that people under stress don't consider things outside themselves (or at least act as if they don't).

Reference

Coke, J. S., Batson, C. D., & McDavis, K. (1978). Empathic mediation of helping: A two-stage model. Journal of Personality and Social Psychology, 36(7), 752–766. DOI: 10.1037/0022-3514.36.7.752

There's more to how much we can support than the amount of natural resources. Infrastructure and technological advance are necessary too. If we do a certain amount of damage to the environment, the opportunity cost is the same as if we fail to build a certain amount of infrastructure, or fail to do a certain amount of scientific research. As such, it is commonly worth while to destroy a certain amount of the environment to help us in other ways.

Consumerism helps none of these, and it would be much better in the long run if we could cut down on it as a society. That being said, we should only worry about saving the environment insomuch as its value will increase fast enough to make up for the the high future value of current investments. If cutting our power consumption in half now will double our economic output in a hundred years due to the greenhouse effect, it's not worth it. If it will multiply it by a factor of 10 in a thousand years, it's not even worth considering.

I just made those numbers up. I don't know whether we exploit the environment too much or too little. I'm just pointing out that it is possible to exploit it too little.

Also, none of this takes into account the intrinsic value of the environment. I should point out that it's not necessarily positive. There is a lot of suffering going on in the wild.
