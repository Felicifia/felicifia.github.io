Hi Com4rade.

I'd say the most important thing to recognise is that a great many objections to utilitarianism demonstrate it to be unintuitive in various wild and wonderful scenarios. But few, if any objections demonstrate objections to utilitarianism demonstrate it to be unintuitive in realistic scenarios.

Do utilitarians evaluate expected consequences or incidental ones? Well, all suffering is bad. On the other hand, there's no point losing sleep over suffering that is unpredictable and random. That stuff is unpreventable. We should basically evaluate suffering that we can prevent. So yeah, I'd rather say that utilitarians maximise expected consequences than incidental ones.

Although I don't have citations for either of those points, I think they follow logically from the idea of maximising happiness and I'm happy to elaborate them.

I second Arepo's suggestion of links.
